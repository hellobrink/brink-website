So you want to edit
the Brink website

Good news: you don't need to know how it's built. Most of the time you're just filling in forms. This guide covers how to get in, what you can change, and how your edits reach the world.

☕ Written for the Brink team

Two ways in

You're either editing, or building

There are two ways to change the site. Nearly all the time, you only need the first.

The Editor

For content

Change wording, swap an image, add a blog post, update a bio, tweak a case study. You do it in a friendly form-based editor (the "CMS") that runs in your browser. No code, nothing to install.

This is you, most days.

The Builder

For pages & design

Build a brand-new page, change a layout, or restyle something. This happens through a conversation with Claude, which writes the code for you and shows you a preview.

For bigger changes — or ask Rob.

Step one

Getting into the editor

  1. Go to the editor

    Open hellobrink.co/admin in your browser. ← address confirmed at launch

  2. Sign in with GitHub

    Click Sign in with GitHub and use your own account. First time only: ask Rob to add you as an editor — a one-off invite that takes him a minute.

  3. You're in

    You'll land on a list of everything you can edit — blog posts, team, case studies and the rest. Pick one and go.

Why GitHub? It's what keeps a tidy record of every change and who made it — so nothing is ever lost, and anything can be undone. You sign in with your own account, not a shared password.

The map

What you can change — and what you can't

If you can't find something in the editor, it's in the right-hand column. That's a job for the Builder (or Rob).

  • Blog posts
  • Team members & their bios

  • Brand-new pages

Step two

Making an edit

  1. Pick what to edit

    Choose a section (say Blog posts), then the entry you want to change — or hit New to add one.

  2. Fill in the fields

    Type into the boxes. To add or replace a picture, drag it straight in. (For images, just upload — you don't need to worry about web addresses or file paths.)

  3. Hit Save

    That's it. The site rebuilds itself in a minute or two and your change appears.

Saving doesn't break anything. Every version is kept, and each change is recorded on its own — so there's always a clean way to undo, right back to how things were.

Step three

From your screen to the world

Edits don't go straight to the public site. They pass through a preview first, so nothing goes live before it's ready.

01

You save

Your change lands on the staging site — a private copy only the team sees.

02

Quick look

Anyone can check it on staging and make sure it looks right.

03

Publish

One step promotes it to live on hellobrink.co.

Being set up for launch
The staging → live safety step is part of the go-live work. Until then, edits appear straight on the preview site — which is fine, because the public site is still the old one for now.

The other way

Building with Claude

For anything that isn't in the editor — a new page, a new section, a design change — you describe what you want to Claude, and it writes the code.

  1. Open the project in Claude

    A one-time setup on your computer. Rob can get you started, or run it for you.

  2. Describe it in plain English

    "Add a page about our new programme, with a hero image and three columns." No jargon needed.

  3. Review the preview

    Claude builds it and shows you how it looks. Ask for changes until it's right.

  4. Publish it the same way

    Through staging, then live — exactly like an editor change.

This is the more advanced route. You don't write code, but you do need the project set up on your machine. If that sounds like a lot, just ask Rob — or drop the idea in the team channel and we'll pick it up.

If in doubt

A few golden rules

  • 1
    Small changes, one at a time.

    Easier to check, and easier to undo if you change your mind.

  • 2
    Nothing is permanent.

    Every version is saved. Anything can be rolled back to how it was.

  • 3
    Preview before you publish.

    That's exactly what staging is for — use it.

  • 4
    When unsure, ask.

    A quick question to Rob beats a guess every time.
